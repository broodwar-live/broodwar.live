A reference of common terms used in the StarCraft: Brood War competitive community. Whether you are new to watching pro BW or brushing up on jargon, this glossary covers the essentials.

---

## A

**Aggression** — An offensive posture or playstyle that prioritizes attacking the opponent over economic development. Aggressive builds sacrifice long-term economy for short-term pressure.

**All-in** — A strategy that commits all available resources to a single attack, with no fallback plan. If the attack fails, the player is almost certainly behind and likely to lose. Examples include proxy Barracks rushes and one-base Reaver pushes.

**APM** — Actions Per Minute. A measure of how many commands a player issues per minute. Top Korean pros regularly exceed 300+ APM. Raw APM includes all inputs, including redundant or wasted actions.

## B

**Bio** — Short for "biological." Refers to a Terran army composition built around infantry units (Marine, Medic, Firebat) rather than mechanical units. Bio play emphasizes mobility and Stim Pack micro.

**BM** — Bad Manners. Disrespectful behavior during a game, such as refusing to say "GG" before leaving, excessive taunting, or intentionally prolonging a won game.

**Build Order** — The specific sequence and timing of structures, units, and upgrades a player follows in the early game. Build orders are often expressed as supply counts (e.g., "9 pool" means Spawning Pool at 9 supply). A good build order provides an efficient opening that transitions into a coherent mid-game strategy.

**Bunker Rush** — A Terran cheese strategy where Barracks and Bunkers are built aggressively near the opponent's base to trap them with Marines early on. Often combined with an SCV pull.

## C

**Cheese** — An unconventional, high-risk/high-reward strategy that relies on surprise to win. If scouted, cheese usually fails badly. Examples: cannon rush, proxy Barracks, 4-pool.

**Choke Point** — A narrow passage on the map where armies must funnel through. Choke points are critical for defensive play — Lurkers, Siege Tanks, and Zealots are especially effective at holding chokes.

**Contain** — A strategy where a player positions their army outside the opponent's base to prevent them from expanding or moving out, while the containing player secures economic advantages elsewhere.

**Control Group** — A set of units or buildings bound to a hotkey (numbers 1-0) for quick selection. Efficient control grouping is fundamental to high-level play.

**Counter** — A unit, composition, or strategy that is particularly effective against another. For example, Corsairs counter Mutalisks, and Dark Swarm counters ranged units.

## D

**Dark Swarm** — A Defiler spell that creates a cloud under which ranged attacks cannot hit ground units. One of the most impactful spells in the game, Dark Swarm enables Zerg ground armies to close distance against Terran bio or Protoss Dragoons.

**Defiler** — A Tier 3 Zerg spellcaster with access to Dark Swarm, Plague, and Consume. Widely considered one of the most powerful and game-defining units in Brood War.

**DPS** — Damage Per Second. A measure of how much damage a unit or group of units can deal over time. Important for evaluating army compositions and cost-efficiency.

**Drop** — Using a transport unit (Dropship, Shuttle, or Overlord) to deliver units into the opponent's base or an undefended location, bypassing the main army. Drops are a core harassment technique.

## E

**EAPM** — Effective Actions Per Minute. APM with redundant or meaningless actions filtered out (such as spam-clicking or repeated selections). EAPM is a more accurate measure of actual decision-making speed.

**Economy** — A player's resource income, determined by the number of workers mining minerals and gas, and the number of active bases. A stronger economy allows producing more units and upgrades.

**ELO** — A rating system originally designed for chess, adapted for competitive BW to rank players based on match results. Higher ELO indicates a stronger player.

**Expand** — To build an additional base (Command Center, Nexus, or Hatchery) at a new resource location. Expanding increases resource income but requires investment and is vulnerable to timing attacks.

## F

**Fast Expand (FE)** — An opening strategy that prioritizes taking a second base early, sacrificing early military strength for long-term economic advantage. Common in most matchups at the pro level.

**FFE** — Forge Fast Expand. A Protoss opening in PvZ where a Forge is built before Gateway, using Photon Cannons to defend the natural expansion while teching up safely.

**FPV** — First-Person View. A broadcast perspective that shows the game from a single player's point of view, including their camera movements, selections, and actions. FPV reveals a player's micro and decision-making in real time.

## G

**Gateway** — The primary Protoss production building for ground units. "Gateway" can also refer to a Gateway-heavy army composition emphasizing Zealots and Dragoons.

**Gas Steal** — Building an Assimilator or Extractor on the opponent's Vespene Geyser to deny them gas income. A common early-game harassment tactic, especially in PvP.

**GG** — Good Game. Said by the losing player before surrendering. Leaving without saying GG is considered bad manners in competitive BW culture.

## H

**Harass** — Attacking the opponent's economy, production, or expansions with a small force, forcing them to divert attention and units from their main strategy. Mutalisk harass and drop play are classic examples.

**High Ground** — Elevated terrain that provides a defensive advantage. Units on high ground cannot be seen by units on low ground without detection, and ranged attacks from low ground to high ground have a chance to miss.

**Hive Tech** — The final tier of Zerg technology, unlocked by morphing a Lair into a Hive. Grants access to Defilers, Ultralisks, Greater Spire, and key upgrades like Adrenal Glands.

## K

**KR** — Abbreviation for Korean. Used to refer to the Korean BW scene, Korean servers (Fish Server), or Korean players. Korea has historically been the center of competitive Brood War.

## L

**Late Game** — The final phase of a match where both players are on multiple bases with maxed or near-maxed armies and full upgrades. Late-game BW often features the most complex engagements and spellcaster usage.

**Lift-off** — The ability of Terran production buildings (Barracks, Factory, Starport) and Command Centers to lift off the ground and fly to a new location. Used for scouting, relocating, or escaping destruction.

**Lurker Contain** — A Zerg strategy where Lurkers are positioned outside the opponent's base (often at the natural ramp), burrowed to deny expansion and movement. Very effective against Protoss ground armies without Observers.

## M

**Macro** — Short for macromanagement. The skill of managing economy, production, and base-building efficiently. Good macro means constantly producing workers, never getting supply blocked, spending resources as fast as they come in, and expanding at the right times.

**Mech** — Short for "mechanical." Refers to a Terran army composition built around Factory units (Vulture, Siege Tank, Goliath) rather than infantry. Mech play emphasizes positional control and siege lines.

**Meta** — Short for metagame. The current prevailing strategies, builds, and compositions that top players favor. The meta shifts over time as players innovate and find counters.

**Micro** — Short for micromanagement. The skill of controlling individual units or small groups during combat to maximize their effectiveness. Examples include Marine stutter-stepping, Mutalisk stacking, and Reaver-Shuttle micro.

**Mineral Line** — The row of mineral patches at a base where workers harvest. Attacking the mineral line (killing workers) is one of the most effective harassment tactics.

**Mutalisk Harass** — Using Mutalisks to fly into the opponent's base and attack workers, production buildings, or tech structures, then retreating before a strong defense arrives. A defining feature of Zerg play.

## N

**Natural (Expansion)** — The expansion base closest to a player's starting location, usually the first expansion taken. Securing the natural is a key milestone in most standard games.

**Nydus Canal** — A Zerg building (requires Hive) that creates a tunnel between two points on creep, allowing instant unit transport. Used for surprise attacks or rapid reinforcement.

## O

**Observer** — A Protoss air unit produced from the Robotics Facility. Permanently cloaked and serves as the primary Protoss detector. Essential for spotting Lurkers, Dark Templar, and Wraiths.

**One-base** — A strategy or situation where a player remains on a single base. One-base play is either a committed all-in or a sign of being contained. Resources on one base are limited, so one-base strategies must end the game quickly.

**Overkill** — When more damage is dealt to a unit than needed to destroy it, wasting potential damage. Splash damage units like Siege Tanks and Reavers are especially prone to overkill. Managing overkill through good targeting is a high-level skill.

## P

**Proxy** — Building a production structure (usually Barracks, Gateway, or Hatchery) close to the opponent's base to reduce travel time for attacking units. Proxy builds are aggressive and rely on the element of surprise.

**Psi Storm** — A High Templar spell that deals 112 damage over its area of effect. One of the most powerful area-damage abilities in the game. Multiple Psi Storms do not stack damage.

**Push** — A concentrated military attack aimed at breaking through the opponent's defenses or taking map control. A push is more deliberate than harass but less committal than an all-in.

## R

**Rally Point** — The waypoint where newly produced units automatically move after being created. Setting rally points to strategic locations ensures reinforcements arrive where needed.

**Ramp** — The incline connecting low ground to high ground on a map. Ramps are natural choke points and critical defensive positions. Controlling the ramp to your natural is fundamental in many matchups.

**Rush** — An early aggressive strategy designed to attack the opponent before they can fully establish their economy or defenses. The 4-pool Zergling rush and proxy Barracks are classic examples.

**Rush Distance** — The physical distance between players' starting locations on a map. Shorter rush distances favor aggressive builds; longer rush distances favor economic play.

## S

**Scout** — Sending a unit (usually a worker) to the opponent's base to gather information about their build order and strategy. Early scouting is essential for making informed decisions.

**Siege** — The Siege Tank's deployed mode, which deals massive splash damage at long range but renders the tank immobile. "Sieging up" is a key part of Terran positional play.

**Sim-city** — The art of placing buildings in optimal positions, especially at choke points or natural expansions, to create walls or maximize efficiency. Named after the city-building game. Protoss Forge-expands and Terran wall-offs both require good sim-city.

**Splash** — Area-of-effect damage that hits multiple units at once. Siege Tanks, Reavers, Psi Storm, and Lurkers all deal splash damage. Splash is especially effective against tightly grouped armies.

**Stim** — Short for Stim Pack. A Terran Academy research that allows Marines and Firebats to sacrifice 10 HP for a temporary boost to movement and attack speed. Stimmed Marines are the cornerstone of Terran bio play.

**Supply Block** — A situation where a player cannot produce more units because they have reached their current supply cap and have not built enough supply structures (Supply Depots, Pylons, or Overlords). Supply blocks waste production time and resources.

## T

**Tech** — Short for technology. Refers to advancing up the tech tree by building prerequisite structures and unlocking new units or upgrades. "Teching up" means investing in higher-tier buildings.

**Third Base** — The third expansion a player takes, usually the second base beyond the natural. Taking a third is a sign of committing to a macro-oriented game plan.

**Timing Attack** — An attack launched at a specific moment when the attacker has a temporary advantage — often right after a key upgrade finishes or before the opponent's tech comes online. Timing attacks exploit windows of opportunity.

**Turtle** — A defensive playstyle where a player stays on a small number of bases, builds heavy static defenses, and avoids engaging until they reach a powerful late-game composition. Turtling is generally considered passive and risky because the opponent can expand freely.

## U

**Unit Composition** — The mix of different unit types in a player's army. Good compositions synergize well (e.g., Marines + Medics + Siege Tanks) and counter what the opponent is building.

## W

**Wall-off** — Using buildings to block a choke point or ramp, preventing enemy units from entering. Terran commonly walls off with Supply Depots and Barracks at their ramp. Protoss can wall with Gateways and Pylons.

**Worker Rush** — Sending all workers to attack the opponent at the very start of the game. A desperation cheese that occasionally works if the opponent is not paying attention.

**Wraparound** — A tactical maneuver where an army approaches from multiple angles simultaneously, surrounding the opponent's forces. Wraparounds negate choke point advantages and are especially effective with melee units like Zealots and Zerglings.
